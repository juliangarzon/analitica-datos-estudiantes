Part 1: Statistics Refresher

The fundamental concepts you need to analyze data

Duration: 45 minutes

Population vs Sample

The Soup Tasting Analogy

You want to know if your soup is salty enough. Do you drink the entire pot? No! You taste one spoonful.

Population = The entire pot of soup (all the data that exists)

Sample = One spoonful (the data you actually measure)

Population

  • All water consumers in Colombia
  • Every transaction ever made
  • All students in all universities

Usually impossible or impractical to measure

Sample

  • 1,000 surveyed consumers
  • Transactions from last year
  • Students in this classroom

What we use to ESTIMATE the population

Key insight: Your sample must be REPRESENTATIVE. A spoonful from the top of the pot may not represent the whole soup!

Measures of Central Tendency

Three ways to answer: "What's a typical value?"

Mean (Average)

sum(values) / count

Add all values, divide by count

Median (Middle)

middle value when sorted

The center value in order

Mode (Most Common)

most frequent value

The value that appears most

Example: Water consumption (m3) for 7 households

Values:   8,  10,  11,  12,  12,  14,  45

Mean:    (8+10+11+12+12+14+45) / 7 = 16.0 m3
Median:  8, 10, 11, [12], 12, 14, 45 = 12.0 m3
Mode:    12 (appears twice)

Notice: Mean (16.0) is pulled up by the outlier (45). Median (12.0) is more representative!

Mean vs Median: The Salary Example

Imagine a Company with 10 Employees

9 employees earn $50,000 per year. The CEO earns $5,000,000.

Mean Salary

$545,000

($50K x 9 + $5M) / 10

"Our average salary is $545K!"

Misleading and unrepresentative

Median Salary

$50,000

Middle value of sorted salaries

"A typical salary is $50K"

Representative of most employees

Rule of Thumb

If mean != median, your data is skewed. Use median for better representation.

Variance and Standard Deviation

Measuring how spread out your data is

The Archery Analogy

Two archers shoot 10 arrows each at a target.

Archer A: All arrows land close together (low standard deviation = consistent)

Archer B: Arrows all over the place (high standard deviation = inconsistent)

Variance (squared units)

variance = avg(distance from mean)^2

How far each value is from the mean, squared

Units: m3^2 (hard to interpret)

Standard Deviation (original units)

std = sqrt(variance)

Same as variance, but in original units

Units: m3 (easy to interpret!)

Visual: Low vs High Standard Deviation

Low std (consistent)

High std (spread out)

The 68-95-99.7 Rule

For normally distributed data

68%

Within 1 std of mean

95%

Within 2 std of mean

99.7%

Within 3 std of mean

Example: If mean water consumption = 12 m3 and std = 3 m3, then 95% of households consume between 6 m3 and 18 m3.

Percentiles and Quartiles

Dividing your data into meaningful segments

The Test Score Analogy

"You scored in the 90th percentile" means you performed better than 90% of test-takers.

It does NOT mean you got 90% of questions correct!

Quartiles: Dividing Data into 4 Parts

Q1

25th percentile

25% below

Q2

50th percentile

= Median

Q3

75th percentile

75% below

IQR

Q3 - Q1

Middle 50%

Statistics in Python

import pandas as pd import numpy as np # Load water consumption data df = pd.read_csv('water_consumption.csv') # Central tendency mean = df['consumption'].mean() # Average median = df['consumption'].median() # Middle value mode = df['consumption'].mode() # Most frequent # Spread std = df['consumption'].std() # Standard deviation var = df['consumption'].var() # Variance # Percentiles and quartiles q1 = df['consumption'].quantile(0.25) q2 = df['consumption'].quantile(0.50) # = median q3 = df['consumption'].quantile(0.75) iqr = q3 - q1 # All at once with describe() df['consumption'].describe()

Part 2: GroupBy - Your First EDA Tool

Now that you know the stats, let's look at them BY GROUP

Split - Apply - Combine

What is GroupBy?

The M&Ms Analogy

Imagine you have a bag of M&Ms. You want to know: which color has the most candies?

Step 1: Sort them into piles by color (SPLIT)

Step 2: Count each pile (APPLY)

Step 3: Compare the results (COMBINE)

GroupBy = Split + Apply + Combine

Original bag:  [R, B, G, R, Y, B, R, G, B, R, Y, G]

SPLIT by color:
  Red:    [R, R, R, R]
  Blue:   [B, B, B]
  Green:  [G, G, G]
  Yellow: [Y, Y]

APPLY count:
  Red: 4, Blue: 3, Green: 3, Yellow: 2

COMBINE into a table:
  Color  | Count
  -------|------
  Red    |  4
  Blue   |  3
  Green  |  3
  Yellow |  2

GroupBy answers: "How does [statistic] vary across [groups]?"

Mean consumption BY stratum. Total billing BY department. Count BY use type.

GroupBy in Python: The Basics

Three simple operations: .mean(), .sum(), .count()

# GroupBy basic pattern: # df.groupby('column_to_split_by')['column_to_measure'].operation() # Average consumption BY stratum/category df.groupby('ESTRATO')['CONSUMO_ACUEDUCTO'].mean() # Total consumption BY municipality df.groupby('MUNICIPIO')['CONSUMO_ACUEDUCTO'].sum() # Count records BY stratum df.groupby('ESTRATO')['CONSUMO_ACUEDUCTO'].count()

The Pattern

df.groupby('GROUP')['VALUE'].operation()

  • GROUP = what to split by
  • VALUE = what to measure
  • operation = how to summarize

Common Operations

  • .mean() - average per group
  • .sum() - total per group
  • .count() - how many per group
  • .median() - middle value per group

GroupBy with Water Consumption Data

Real questions answered with one line of code

# Question: Which stratum/category consumes the most water? df.groupby('ESTRATO')['CONSUMO_ACUEDUCTO'].mean() # ESTRATO # Industrial 45,320.3 # Comercial 12,450.7 # Estrato6 3,180.5 # Question: Which municipality has the most subscribers? df.groupby('MUNICIPIO')['SUSCRIPTORES_ACUEDUCTO'].sum().sort_values(ascending=False).head(5) # Question: What is the median consumption per stratum? df.groupby('ESTRATO')['CONSUMO_ACUEDUCTO'].median()
Why GroupBy matters for EDA: Instead of one mean for the entire dataset, you get one mean per group. This reveals patterns you would never see in a single number. "Average consumption is 5,000 m3" hides the fact that industrial users consume 20x more than residential users.
Tip: Add .sort_values(ascending=False) to see the largest groups first. Add .head(10) to see just the top 10.

Understanding ONE variable at a time

Distributions, Outliers, Visualizations

What is Univariate Analysis?

Univariate = ONE Variable at a Time

Like checking your height without comparing to anyone else

The 5-Step Univariate Framework

1

What type?

Numeric or Categorical

2

Distribution?

Shape of the data

3

Center?

Mean or Median

4

Spread?

Std, IQR, Range

5

Outliers?

Unusual values

Remember: Understand each variable individually BEFORE looking at relationships between variables (bivariate analysis).

Understanding Distributions

A distribution shows how values are spread across a range

Normal (Bell Curve)

Most values near center

Example: Heights, test scores

Right Skewed

Long tail to the right

Example: Income, house prices

Bimodal

Two peaks

Example: Mixed populations

Why Distribution Shape Matters

It determines which statistics to use (mean vs median) and what patterns to expect

Skewness: Which Way Does the Tail Point?

Right (Positive) Skew

Mean > Median

Tail points RIGHT

Income, water consumption, prices

Left (Negative) Skew

Mean < Median

Tail points LEFT

Age at death, exam scores (ceiling)

Remember: The mean gets "pulled" toward the tail. So:
- Right skew: mean > median (mean pulled right)
- Left skew: mean < median (mean pulled left)

Outliers: The Giraffes in the Dog Park

The Outlier Analogy

Imagine a dog park with Chihuahuas, Beagles, Labradors, and one Giraffe.

The giraffe is an outlier. It stands out because it doesn't belong to the normal group.

What Causes Outliers?

  • Data entry errors - Typo: 150 instead of 15
  • Measurement errors - Broken sensor
  • True anomalies - A water leak causing high consumption
  • Different population - Industrial vs residential

Why They Matter

  • Distort statistics - Pull mean away from center
  • Break models - ML models can be sensitive
  • Hide patterns - Compress visualization scales
  • OR reveal insights! - Fraud detection

Detecting Outliers: The IQR Method

The 1.5 x IQR Rule

# Outlier detection with IQR method q1 = df['consumption'].quantile(0.25) q3 = df['consumption'].quantile(0.75) iqr = q3 - q1 lower_bound = q1 - 1.5 * iqr upper_bound = q3 + 1.5 * iqr # Find outliers outliers = df[(df['consumption'] < lower_bound) | (df['consumption'] > upper_bound)] print(f"Found {len(outliers)} outliers out of {len(df)} records")

Visualization: Histograms

The go-to chart for understanding distributions

What is a Histogram?

A histogram divides values into "bins" and counts how many fall in each bin.

What to Look For

  • Shape: Normal? Skewed? Bimodal?
  • Center: Where is the peak?
  • Spread: How wide is the distribution?
  • Outliers: Bars far from the rest?
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt # Basic histogram df['consumption'].hist(bins=30, edgecolor='black') plt.xlabel('Water Consumption (m3)') plt.ylabel('Frequency') plt.title('Distribution of Water Consumption') plt.show() # With seaborn (nicer) import seaborn as sns sns.histplot(df['consumption'], bins=30, kde=True)

Visualization: Box Plots

The 5-number summary in one picture

import matplotlib.pyplot as plt import seaborn as sns # Basic box plot df['consumption'].plot(kind='box') plt.title('Water Consumption Distribution') plt.show() # Horizontal box plot with seaborn sns.boxplot(x=df['consumption']) plt.xlabel('Water Consumption (m3)') plt.show()

Decision Guide: Mean vs Median

Situation Use Mean Use Median
Distribution is symmetric Yes Also fine
Distribution is skewed No Yes
Data has outliers No Yes
Need mathematical properties Yes Limited
Income/price data Rarely Usually

Quick Check

Compare mean and median. If they are very different, your data is probably skewed and you should use the median!

Putting It All Together: Complete Univariate Analysis

import pandas as pd import matplotlib.pyplot as plt # Step 1: Load and understand the variable df = pd.read_csv('water_consumption.csv') print("Data type:", df['consumption'].dtype) print("Non-null count:", df['consumption'].count()) # Step 2: Summary statistics print(df['consumption'].describe()) # Step 3: Check for skewness mean = df['consumption'].mean() median = df['consumption'].median() print(f"Mean: {mean:.2f}, Median: {median:.2f}") print(f"Skewness: {'Right skewed' if mean > median else 'Left skewed or symmetric'}") # Step 4: Visualize fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 2, figsize=(12, 4)) df['consumption'].hist(bins=30, ax=axes[0]) df['consumption'].plot(kind='box', ax=axes[1]) plt.tight_layout() plt.show() # Step 5: Detect outliers q1, q3 = df['consumption'].quantile([0.25, 0.75]) iqr = q3 - q1 outliers = df[(df['consumption'] < q1 - 1.5*iqr) | (df['consumption'] > q3 + 1.5*iqr)] print(f"Outliers: {len(outliers)} ({len(outliers)/len(df)*100:.1f}%)")
