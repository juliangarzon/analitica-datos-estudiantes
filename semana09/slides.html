Tufte's Data-Ink Ratio

*

Edward Tufte: "The best visualization is one where removing any element would reduce the data communicated."

The Principle:

Data-Ink Ratio = Ink used for data / Total ink used

Goal: Maximize this ratio (get it as close to 1 as possible)

Non-Data Ink (Remove It)

  • 3D effects and shadows
  • Decorative gridlines
  • Background images
  • Excessive borders
  • Redundant labels

Data Ink (Keep It)

  • Bars, lines, points (the data itself)
  • Axis labels (minimum needed)
  • Clear title
  • Legend (only if necessary)
  • Key annotations
CONTENT

Data-Ink Ratio: Before and After

Low Data-Ink Ratio

Ratio: ~30% data ink

High Data-Ink Ratio

Ratio: ~90% data ink

Tufte's Test: Look at your chart. Can you remove anything without losing data? If yes, remove it.

The 5-Second Rule

If someone cannot understand your chart in 5 seconds, it is too complex.

What Should Be Instant

  • What am I looking at? (title)
  • What is being compared? (x-axis)
  • What is being measured? (y-axis)
  • What is the main takeaway?

Signs of Complexity

  • More than 5-7 categories
  • Multiple y-axes
  • Legend with 10+ items
  • Need to read fine print

The Test

Show your chart to someone for 5 seconds, then hide it. Ask them: "What did you see?"

If they cannot answer, simplify.

Choosing the Right Chart

*

Chart selection is like choosing transportation: You would not take a bicycle to cross an ocean or a plane to go next door.

Question You Are Answering Chart Type Example
How do categories compare? Bar chart Cases by age group
How did something change over time? Line chart Monthly cases 2020-2024
What is the relationship between two things? Scatter plot Age vs. report delay
What is the distribution? Histogram Distribution of victim ages
What are the parts of a whole? Pie / Stacked bar Types of abuse (only if 2-5 parts!)

Start with the question

Do not ask which chart looks nice. Ask what comparison, trend, relationship, or part-to-whole message you need to make visible.

What changes in this class

Bar Charts: The Workhorse

Use for comparing discrete categories. The most versatile chart type.

Question: Which category has more cases?

Example: Cases by age group

What weakens the message

  • Random order hides ranking.
  • Rainbow colors add noise, not meaning.
  • A truncated axis exaggerates small gaps.

What this chart says well

  • Fast category comparison.
  • Clear ranking after sorting.
  • One highlight color points to the key group.

When to use horizontal bars: When category names are long or when you have many categories (easier to read).

CONTENT

Line Charts: Showing Trends

Use for continuous data over time. The line implies a connection between points.

Question: How did cases change over time?

Example: Monthly cases, Jan to Jun

Where line charts go wrong

  • Ten lines at once become spaghetti.
  • Truncated y-axis dramatizes a mild change.
  • A line over categories suggests false continuity.

What this chart says well

  • Direction and speed of change.
  • Turning points across time.
  • Trend is easier to see than in a table.

Warning: A line implies continuity. Do not use lines for categorical data (months as categories) unless you mean to show the trend.

CONTENT

Scatter Plots: Finding Relationships

Use to show the relationship between two numeric variables.

Question: Are these two variables moving together?

Example: Age vs. report delay

What to inspect first

  • Positive, negative, or no relationship.
  • Clusters that suggest subgroups.
  • Outliers worth investigating.

Best practice in one sentence

  • Add a trend line only if it helps.
  • Use transparency when points overlap.
  • Never sell correlation as causation.

Remember: Correlation does not imply causation. Ice cream sales and drownings both increase in summer, but ice cream does not cause drowning!

CONTENT

Pie Charts: Use With Extreme Caution

*

Pie charts are the most misused chart type. Humans are bad at comparing angles and areas.

Same data: 35%, 33%, 32%

Why this is hard to compare in a pie chart

When pie charts fail

  • Similar slices hide precise differences.
  • More than 4-5 parts overload attention.
  • Comparing several pies is even worse.

When pie charts still work

  • Very few categories.
  • One slice clearly dominates.
  • The message is majority share, not exact rank.

Alternative: Consider a stacked bar chart or waffle chart for part-to-whole relationships with more categories.

The Same Data Can Tell Different Stories

Changing the chart does not change the numbers, but it changes what your audience notices first.

Pie chart first impression

Good for "part of a whole", weak for close comparisons.

What it says loudly: Health is the biggest slice.

Bar chart first impression

Same data, but now ranking and distance are easier to compare.

What it says loudly: The gap between categories is obvious.

Analyst rule

Choose the chart that makes the intended comparison easiest to see, not the chart that merely can contain the data.

Color Meanings and Accessibility

Cultural Associations

 Blue Trust, calm, professional
Green Good, growth, go
Red Danger, error, stop
Orange/Yellow Warning, attention
Gray Neutral, secondary

Colorblind Accessibility

8% of men have some form of color blindness.

  • Never rely on color ALONE
  • Avoid red-green as only differentiator
  • Use patterns, labels, or shapes too
  • Test with colorblind simulators

Safe palette: Blue + Orange is colorblind-friendly. Blue + Gray for emphasis is always safe.

The Visualization Checklist

Before sharing any chart, verify:

Content

  • Title tells the story (not just describes)
  • Axes are labeled clearly
  • Data source is cited
  • Units are specified
  • Right chart type for the question

Design

  • Passes the 5-second rule
  • High data-ink ratio
  • Color used meaningfully
  • No 3D effects or chartjunk
  • Accessible for colorblind

Good Title vs. Bad Title

Bad: "Cases by Year"

(Describes the chart)

Good: "Cases doubled from 2020 to 2023"

(Tells the story)

Class Discussion: Obvious Tradeoffs

Start with the easy cases. Students usually detect these fast, and that builds confidence before the subtle examples.

1. Pie vs bar

Same data: 52%, 23%, 17%, 8%

Use when You want to show a dominant majority.
Avoid when You need precise comparison between close values.

2. Bar vs lollipop

Same ranking: both work, but not with the same force.

Bar Better for magnitude and beginners.
Lollipop Lighter visually, but can weaken magnitude reading.

3. Line vs bars over time

Both can be valid for monthly data, but they answer slightly different classroom questions.

Line Better for trend and turning points.
Bars Better for exact month-by-month totals.

4. Stacked vs grouped bar

Same subgroup data: one emphasizes total, the other subgroup comparison.

Stacked Better when total matters most.
Grouped Better when comparing subgroups across categories.
SUMMARY

Class Discussion: Not-So-Obvious Tradeoffs

Now move to cases where the choice is less obvious, or where both charts are technically acceptable but communicate different things.

5. Full axis vs truncated axis

Same bars, same values, different emotional impact.

Full axis Honest visual proportion in bar charts.
Truncated Can exaggerate modest differences and push a narrative.

6. Histogram vs boxplot

Same distribution, different emphasis.

Histogram Better for shape, skew, and modality.
Boxplot Better summary, but hides sample shape and counts.

7. Scatter vs hexbin / density

Classic `data-to-viz` overplotting problem.

Scatter Great for few points and outliers.
Hexbin / density Better when many points overlap and hide structure.

8. Treemap vs sorted bar

Same category totals, different reading task.

Treemap Compact for many categories and hierarchy.
Sorted bar Better when exact ranking is the classroom goal.

Real Example: Same Category Data, Different Reading Task

Data to Viz repeatedly shows this point: when category ranking matters, some charts are simply easier to read than others.

Lollipop chart

Looks lighter and more elegant, but the line-to-dot encoding can weaken instant magnitude reading.

Sorted bar chart

Heavier visually, but clearer when the audience must compare category sizes fast.

Source: Data to Viz, “Lollipop chart” and “Barplot”

Real Example: Subgroups, Totals, and Comparison

This is another frequent real design choice. The data do not change, but the best chart depends on whether you want to emphasize totals or subgroup comparison.

Stacked bar chart

Good for total height, weaker for comparing the middle segments across categories.

Grouped bar chart

Harder to read the total, but much better when the teaching goal is subgroup comparison.

Source: Data to Viz, “Barplot”

Real Dataset: Total Emissions vs Emissions Per Person

Using the same public dataset, the story changes depending on whether you show absolute emissions or emissions per capita. Both are real, but they answer different questions.

Total annual CO2 emissions

Best when the question is scale: who emits the most in total?

What students see: China dominates in total volume.

CO2 emissions per capita

Best when the question is average footprint per person.

What students see: the US looks much larger once the denominator changes.

Source: Our World in Data, “Annual CO₂ emissions” and “CO₂ emissions per capita”

Absolute levels over time
